*The EFF argues that weaponizing the Wayback Machine against machine learning threatens humanity's historical record*
▲ 489 pts  ·  137 comments

As the Internet Archive faces mounting pressure from publishers and rights holders seeking to exclude their content from AI training datasets, the Electronic Frontier Foundation has issued a stark warning: the cure may be worse than the disease. In a substantive analysis that garnered 489 upvotes, the EFF argues that aggressively blocking the Archive's crawlers—ostensibly to prevent AI companies from scraping copyrighted material—would effectively dismantle the web's institutional memory. The tension exposes a collision between two competing interests: protecting creators' rights and preserving humanity's digital heritage.

The argument hinges on a crucial asymmetry: AI companies can pursue alternative training data sources and commercial licensing agreements, but the Internet Archive's mission—maintaining a comprehensive historical record of human knowledge and culture—has no backup plan. Once access is revoked, that erasure becomes permanent. The EFF's position doesn't dismiss copyright concerns wholesale, but rather frames the question as one of proportion and alternatives. Preventing the Archive from serving its archival function in order to reduce AI training data availability is akin to burning libraries to stop photocopying.

The 137 comments reflect genuine philosophical disagreement about whether the internet should be treated as a commons to be preserved or a commodified space where access must be continually negotiated and monetized. But the underlying stakes are unambiguous: the decision made in the next eighteen months will determine whether future generations inherit a fragmented, commercialized internet or a more complete historical record.

Child Safety Law Becomes Tool for Internet Censorship, Advocates Warn

*Well-intentioned protection legislation risks becoming infrastructure for sweeping content control*
▲ 482 pts  ·  241 comments

Under the banner of protecting minors, governments and platforms are quietly consolidating control mechanisms that could restrict internet access for entire populations. The Dyne Foundation's analysis of child protection legislation—which sparked 482 upvotes and 241 comments—argues that well-intentioned safety laws are being weaponized as frameworks for censorship that extend far beyond protecting children from genuine harms. The slippage is worth examining: age verification systems, content filtering, and parental monitoring tools, once positioned as surgical instruments for harm reduction, increasingly function as infrastructure for broader access control.

The concern isn't hypothetical. Several jurisdictions have begun implementing blanket content restrictions under child protection mandates, effectively determining what adults can access based on a lowest-common-denominator approach to "safe for children." Search engines optimize for algorithmic age-appropriateness, payment processors block certain legitimate merchants, and platforms institute friction that burdens entire user bases. The Dyne analysis suggests this represents a fundamental category error: conflating the protection of children with the surveillance and control of the broader population. The two are not equivalent challenges and require different solutions.

The spirited engagement in the comments suggests developers and policy observers recognize the danger of embedding control mechanisms in infrastructure under benevolent pretexts. History suggests such mechanisms, once installed, rarely disappear; they persist and expand. The question animating the discussion is whether effective child safety requires systems that impact only those we're trying to protect—or whether we've accepted broad collateral damage to general internet freedom as the acceptable cost.
