Bryan Keller did something that shouldn't exist: he made Mac OS X run on a Nintendo Wii. Not a simulation, not a joke, not some theoretical exercise. A genuine port of Apple's operating system to a gaming console from 2006, complete enough to prove viability. The technical chasm is almost comical to contemplate—a PowerPC processor designed for living room entertainment, a gigabyte of RAM, and an operating system built for multi-core modern Macs. Yet here we are, staring at the impossible made mundane.

The project sits at the intersection of software archaeology and determined brute force. The Wii's PowerPC architecture shares lineage with older Macs, creating a architectural bridge Keller could exploit. But exploiting a bridge requires understanding not just the target system's internals, but the profound mismatch between an OS's assumptions about memory, peripherals, and processing power versus what a 2006 console actually offers. The technical write-up doesn't shy away from the grotesque hacks required: stripping features, reimagining memory maps, essentially negotiating with the hardware about what it's willing to pretend to be.

What's remarkable isn't that this works—it's that someone thought to try, then documented the journey. The project suggests a truth about systems programming that universities often bury: the boundaries between "possible" and "insane" are thinner than they appear, and they're usually defended only by convention, not physics.

*The cult-favorite macOS application finally arrives on Linux—and the security community is paying attention.*
▲ 786 pts  ·  271 comments

Little Snitch, the notoriously obsessive network monitor that has been the paranoid Mac user's constant companion for nearly two decades, has crossed over. The application—famous for its granular per-process network filtering, its library of suspicious behavior patterns, and its user interface that treats every packet like a minor diplomatic incident—now exists for Linux systems. For a security-conscious operating system that's been waiting for a consumer-friendly equivalent, this is the kind of arrival that gets people talking.

The Linux version represents more than just porting code; it's a statement about what security expectations should look like on free operating systems. Little Snitch built its reputation on the principle that users deserve to know, and more importantly, to *control*, what their applications are communicating across the network. It turns a passive activity—network traffic—into an active conversation between user and operating system. Linux systems have had tools like iptables and ufw for decades, but they've always been for administrators, not mortals. Little Snitch translates network security into a language humans can actually read and act upon.

With 271 comments on the announcement, the response signals both hunger for this capability and skepticism about whether a commercial tool can truly belong in the Linux ecosystem. The deeper tension is architectural: can a graphical network monitor compete with the kernel-level flexibility that Linux offers, or will it always feel like an afterthought to distributions built on the principle of total transparency and control?

A 1991 Science Fiction Story About Alien First Contact Haunts Us Still—For All the Right ReasonsshareCopied!

*Terry Bisson's "They're Made Out of Meat" has become a litmus test for how we imagine intelligence and otherness.*
▲ 556 pts  ·  151 comments

Sometimes a story doesn't age; it just becomes more accurate. Terry Bisson's "They're Made Out of Meat," published in 1991, reads like a conversation between two cosmic bureaucrats trying to process something their procedures never anticipated: a civilization made of biological tissue that somehow achieved sentience. The story is twelve minutes at reading speed, maybe 1,400 words on the page. The impact is inversely proportional to its length.

The setup is brilliantly dumb: an alien intelligence discovers Earth and reports their findings to command. The humans are made of meat. All of them. Their thoughts happen in meat. They use meat to communicate, to sense the universe, to reproduce. The aliens find this profoundly and almost morally suspect. Why would any intelligent species persist in such a fragile, vulnerable, temporary form? The conversation escalates in circles—maybe the humans are not sentient at all, perhaps they're just sophisticated biological machines mimicking consciousness. Maybe they deserve preservation? Maybe they deserve warning? Maybe they deserve neither because they're essentially already dead?

What arrives across the decades is something between comedy and dread. Bisson captured something true about how intelligence perceives itself: with a kind of embarrassed certainty that whatever we are, it's the only possible way to be intelligent. The story has become a cultural touchstone—556 points on Hacker News suggests it keeps finding new readers who recognize themselves in the aliens' confusion. In an era when we're building artificial minds and worrying about what they'll think of us, a conversation between creatures baffled by our meat suddenly feels less like 1991 science fiction and more like prophecy.
