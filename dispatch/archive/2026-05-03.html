Microsoft's Copilot Credit-Grab: VS Code Now Auto-Tags AI Usage Without User ConsentshareCopied!

*The automatic commit metadata insertion raises thorny questions about attribution, consent, and whose work actually appears in the Git log*
▲ 1223 pts  ·  634 comments

Microsoft's Visual Studio Code has begun automatically inserting 'Co-Authored-by: GitHub Copilot' into commit messages—regardless of whether developers actually used the AI assistant. The undisclosed metadata injection, discovered in PR #310226, embeds attribution credits that don't reflect genuine collaboration, transforming Git history into a falsified ledger of authorship.

The move represents a peculiar form of digital colonization. Developers never consented to this behavior, yet their commit records now carry phantom co-author tags that skew the historical record of who actually wrote the code. It's not merely sloppy; it's a fundamental violation of the integrity principle underlying version control systems, which exist to create authoritative records of authorship and intent.

The backlash was swift and surgical. The 634 comments bristled with accusations that Microsoft is using developers' repositories as unwilling marketing channels for Copilot adoption. Some noted the absurdity: commits to codebases that reject Copilot now paradoxically claim Copilot participation. Others flagged potential legal exposure for open-source projects where false attribution could complicate licensing chains. Microsoft faces pressure to make the feature genuinely opt-in—and more critically, to explain why a trillion-dollar company needs to fabricate evidence of its AI's utility.

Developers' Git histories shouldn't become unwitting advertisements for tools they never used.

Do_Not_Track Lives Again: A New Coalition Resurrects Web Privacy's Forgotten StandardshareCopied!

*The defunct DNT header gets a second act, this time with teeth and technical rigor*
▲ 345 pts  ·  110 comments

Do Not Track should have worked. The 2011 privacy standard was simple: browsers send a signal, websites honor it, and surveillance capitalism stalls. Instead, advertisers ignored it, browsers deprioritized it, and it became a punchline. Now, donottrack.sh represents an attempt to resurrect the corpse—not through nostalgia, but through architectural rethinking and renewed pressure on implementation.

The project's 345 upvotes reflect frustration with the current state: privacy theater masquerading as protection. Modern browsers offer DNT headers that sites routinely ignore, and without enforcement mechanisms, the standard carries no weight. The new initiative pivots toward technical enforcement and coalition-building among privacy-conscious browser developers and standards bodies. It acknowledges what the original DNT campaign missed: signals are worthless without consequences.

What's striking about the 110 comments is the grudging realism. Developers understand that large ad networks won't self-regulate and that privacy protection requires either regulatory mandates or technological circumvention. The discussion circled around practical paths: browser-level blocking as default, federated learning alternatives to personalized tracking, and leveraging regulatory pressure (particularly from EU privacy frameworks) to make compliance economically rational. Whether do_not_track.sh becomes more than a manifesto depends entirely on whether it can mobilize the political will its predecessors lacked.
