The fantasy has always been seductive: plug a desktop-class graphics card into your thin, sleek MacBook and suddenly you've got a gaming powerhouse. Reality, as documented in a meticulous technical deep-dive on scottjg.com, proves far messier. A creator armed with Nvidia's flagship RTX 5090—a card that costs more than most laptops—connected it to an M4 MacBook Air via Thunderbolt and discovered what enthusiasts have long suspected: the architecture simply doesn't sing the same song.

MacOS gaming exists in a perpetual state of technical compromise. Game developers optimize first for Windows, second for consoles, and treat macOS as an afterthought. When you add an external GPU to the equation, you're introducing Thunderbolt bandwidth bottlenecks, driver fragmentation, and the fundamental mismatch between Apple's Metal API and games built on DirectX. The RTX 5090, a card engineered to demolish 4K gaming benchmarks on Windows, becomes a high-powered ornament tethered to a machine that doesn't know what to do with it.

The 145 comments reveal the deeper tragedy: not that the experiment failed, but that it failed predictably. This is the macOS gaming question posed again and again since 2006. Until game studios treat Apple Silicon as a genuine platform—not a port of a port—external GPUs will remain expensive demonstrations of physics rather than practical gaming solutions.

Privacy Theater: How Mullvad's Exit IPs Became a Fingerprinting GoldmineshareCopied!

*The VPN that promised anonymity accidentally created a new way to track users*
▲ 399 pts  ·  243 comments

Mullvad built its reputation on paranoia—the good kind. No usernames, no passwords, no logs, no corporate overlords. Just a VPN that treated user privacy like an existential cause. So the discovery documented on tmctmt.com that Mullvad's exit IP addresses can be weaponized for fingerprinting represents a particular flavor of irony: a privacy tool undermined by the very architecture designed to protect it.

The vulnerability hinges on a counterintuitive detail. Mullvad rotates its exit IPs regularly, but within windows of time, the pool of available addresses is limited and predictable. By correlating timestamps, geographic patterns, and exit IP sequences, researchers found they could construct a probabilistic fingerprint—a kind of behavioral signature that persists even as individual IPs rotate. It's not unbreakable surveillance, but it's a crack in the facade. An attacker with access to aggregated flow data and timing information could theoretically link disparate browsing sessions to the same user, transforming Mullvad's technical infrastructure into an inadvertent identification mechanism.

The 243 comments oscillate between technical nitpicking and existential despair. Some argue the attack requires unrealistic preconditions; others contend that *any* fingerprinting vector in a privacy-first VPN is a fundamental failure. Mullvad will likely patch, rotate, or redesign. But the episode exposes a deeper truth: perfect anonymity might not be technically achievable—only varying degrees of difficulty.
