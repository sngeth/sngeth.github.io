Project Gutenberg's Quiet Revolution: A Quarter-Million Books, Now Better Than EvershareCopied!

*The digital library that refuses to die keeps adding features that matter to readers and researchers alike*
▲ 680 pts  ·  171 comments

Project Gutenberg, the internet's oldest and most stubborn custodian of public domain literature, is experiencing a renaissance that few noticed arriving. Founded in 1971 when Michael Hart literally typed the Declaration of Independence into a computer, the project has evolved from a fringe digital archive into something far more sophisticated—a full-featured platform that rivals commercial e-book vendors while remaining entirely free and ad-free.

The latest improvements signal a maturation long overdue. Better search functionality, improved metadata, mobile-optimized readers, and integration with tools like Standard Ebooks have transformed Gutenberg from a museum piece into a genuinely useful repository. The project now hosts over 70,000 texts in dozens of languages, and the community continues adding high-quality, carefully proofread editions at a steady clip. Unlike Amazon or Apple's walled gardens, every book here exists in the commons—formatted for every device, DRM-free, and licensed for unfettered access.

What makes this moment significant isn't nostalgia. As major publishers wage war against AI training datasets and restrict digital lending, Gutenberg represents something increasingly radical: a permanent, non-profit alternative that asks nothing of users but engagement. The site's steady improvement reflects a simple truth: when information wants to be free, and volunteers maintain it with care, obstacles crumble.

The AI Delusion Hitting Corporate Boardrooms: Mitchell Hashimoto's Warning About Collective PsychosisshareCopied!

*An open letter from a tech founder exposes how panic and hype are driving catastrophically misaligned AI investments*
▲ 673 pts  ·  302 comments

Mitchell Hashimoto, co-founder of HashiCorp, didn't mince words in a recent Twitter thread that struck a nerve across the tech industry: entire companies are operating under what he calls "AI psychosis"—a collective hallucination where executives, boards, and investors have become so intoxicated by large language model capabilities that they're making decisions divorced from reality.

The diagnosis is damning. Companies are pivoting entire product roadmaps around AI without understanding where these tools actually excel. Product teams are replacing engineers with chatbots. Executives are demanding "AI features" the way they once demanded blockchain, and accountants are signing off on budgets that would make dot-com investors blush. The real problem: nobody's asking whether these implementations solve actual customer problems or merely check a box on investor checklists. Hashimoto's observation cuts to something uncomfortable—that we're witnessing mass organizational delusion, where the emperor has no clothes and everyone pretends not to notice.

The pattern repeats throughout tech history: first comes the genuine innovation, then comes the irrational exuberance, then comes the wreckage. What distinguishes this cycle is its speed and scope. AI's capabilities are real enough to justify excitement, but its limitations are being systematically ignored by boardrooms intoxicated by stock valuations and competitive anxiety. Hashimoto's bluntness suggests the comedown, when it arrives, will be spectacular.

Wikipedia in Retrograde: A Genius Hack Resurrects the Charm of Windows XP NostalgiashareCopied!

*One developer rebuilt Wikipedia as a desktop file browser, and the result is weirdly perfect*
▲ 482 pts  ·  111 comments

In an era when every interface mimics glassy mobile apps and infinite scrolls, Sami Smith released a small act of defiant absurdity: Wikipedia reimagined as a Windows XP desktop file explorer. Click into a category and watch it spawn a new window. Navigate articles like folders. The entire sum of human knowledge, organized as a nested directory tree with beveled buttons and that particular shade of XP gray that haunted a generation's school computer labs.

The brilliance lies not in novelty but in usability. Smith's interface transforms Wikipedia's notoriously overwhelming information architecture into something tangible and spatial. Your brain already understands file navigation; it already knows how to drill down through folders. By treating Wikipedia's category hierarchy as literal folders, the explorer forces deliberate exploration rather than algorithmic recommendation. You move intentionally through knowledge rather than being swept along by recommendation engines and engagement hacks. It's slower. It's stranger. It works.

More importantly, Smith's project is a quiet rebuke to contemporary design orthodoxy—the assumption that every interface must be "modern," must scroll infinitely, must deploy dark patterns and notifications to maximize engagement. Sometimes the solutions that worked in 2005 worked because they were honest. Wikipedia Explorer doesn't try to addict you. It just sits there, patient and functional, waiting for you to close the window.

DOJ's Subpoena Blitz: Government Demands Names of 100,000 Users of Popular Car-Hacking AppshareCopied!

*In a sweeping privacy assault, federal prosecutors are targeting vehicle modification enthusiasts through Apple and Google*
▲ 338 pts  ·  230 comments

The Department of Justice has demanded that Apple and Google hand over identifying information on more than 100,000 users of a car modification application, marking an aggressive escalation in how federal authorities handle digital privacy when regulatory interests align with law enforcement. The app in question allows drivers to diagnose and modify vehicle systems—a practice the DOJ has increasingly targeted as part of what it characterizes as an emissions compliance crackdown, though the move raises immediate questions about proportionality and the chilling effect such mass subpoenas create.

The technical mechanism is straightforward but troubling: both Apple and Google received legal demands not for a handful of suspects but for entire user populations, effectively conducting mass surveillance with a subpoena rather than warrants tied to individual probable cause. The distinction matters. A warrant targeting a specific person requires judicial review of specific evidence. A blanket demand for 100,000 user identities bypasses that scrutiny entirely. The app's primary user base likely includes legitimate mechanics, hobbyists, and vehicle owners conducting lawful repairs—territory long protected by right-to-repair advocates and increasingly by legislation.

What emerges is a pattern worth monitoring: federal agencies increasingly weaponizing platform subpoena powers to identify entire user classes rather than specific bad actors. The emissions angle provides regulatory cover, but the precedent extends far beyond automobiles. If the DOJ can demand 100,000 user identities from a car app today, what's to stop similar demands targeting political organizing tools, encrypted messaging apps, or VPN services tomorrow?

Google's Zero-Click Nightmare: A Complete Exploit Chain Exposes Pixel 10 Users to Silent TakeovershareCopied!

*A devastating vulnerability chain can compromise a device without user interaction—and it's getting worse*
▲ 330 pts  ·  150 comments

Google's Project Zero team disclosed a zero-click exploit chain targeting the Pixel 10 that represents the kind of nightmare scenario security researchers wake up screaming about: a complete pathway from attack vector to full device compromise that requires zero user interaction. No clicking a malicious link. No opening a suspicious attachment. Just existing, and being pwned.

Zero-click vulnerabilities are the Grim Reaper of mobile security because they eliminate the human element—that last line of defense where social engineering typically falters. A user receiving a malicious text can delete it. A suspicious email can go unread. But a zero-click chain works in the background, exploiting kernel-level vulnerabilities in services running invisibly on every device. The Pixel 10 chain, as documented by Project Zero, chains together multiple vulnerabilities across different system components, each one necessary for the full takeover. The attack surface spans media processing, system services, and privilege escalation—the full stack.

What makes this disclosure particularly significant is timing: it arrives as the industry is still grappling with the reality that truly bulletproof mobile security may be impossible. Each patch closes one door, but the architectural vulnerabilities remain. Google will issue patches, users will gradually update, and researchers will find new chains. It's an arms race where the attacker only needs to find one path through the labyrinth; defenders must secure every exit. The Pixel 10 exploit is a reminder that even flagship devices, maintained by trillion-dollar companies with dedicated security teams, are perpetually one vulnerability away from catastrophe.

California's Gaming Lifeline: Landmark Bill Forces Publishers to Patch Games or Face Refunds When Servers DieshareCopied!

*Legislators finally crack down on the video game industry's most profitable scam—making titles unplayable when the money stops*
▲ 281 pts  ·  160 comments

California's legislature took a sledgehammer to one of gaming's most defensible indefensibles: the right to render purchased games unplayable the moment a publisher decides a server isn't profitable anymore. A newly advanced bill would require game publishers to either maintain online functionality indefinitely or offer refunds when servers shut down—a simple principle that's somehow revolutionary in an industry built on digital planned obsolescence.

The scope of the problem is staggering. Publishers have shuttered servers for games ranging from beloved franchises to casual mobile titles, effectively stealing access from millions of players who paid full price for products marketed as complete experiences. These games don't degrade gracefully—they become bricked hardware, unplayable digital ghosts occupying hard drives. Players can't even preserve them through emulation because doing so violates the DMCA. Publishers argue maintenance costs justify the practice, but the real motivation is simpler: killing old games creates urgency to buy new ones. California's bill punches directly at that business model.

What makes this legislative moment consequential is that it treats games as products rather than services—a distinction the industry has fought ferociously to blur. Once a publisher sells you something labeled as a game, California's logic suggests, you own it in meaningful ways. That principle threatens the entire games-as-service ecosystem where player investment depends entirely on publisher goodwill. Whether California can enforce such protections against companies operating globally remains uncertain, but the bill signals that consumer patience with digital disposability is finally exhausted. The question now is whether other states follow—or whether publishers simply restrict sales to California to avoid compliance.
