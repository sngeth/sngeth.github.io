Critical VSCode Flaw Exposes GitHub Tokens to One-Click TheftshareCopied!

*A researcher reveals how a seemingly minor UI quirk becomes a gaping door for attackers to pilfer authentication credentials*
▲ 444 pts  ·  65 comments

A vulnerability disclosed this week in Visual Studio Code demonstrates how a single misplaced trust assumption can transform a beloved development environment into a credential exfiltration pipeline. Security researcher Ammar Askar documented a one-click attack vector allowing malicious actors to steal GitHub authentication tokens directly from the editor, requiring nothing more than social engineering savvy and a user's momentary inattention.

The flaw exploits VSCode's integration with the GitHub authentication system, where tokens—the keys to repository access, CI/CD pipelines, and organizational secrets—flow through the application with insufficient friction for extraction. The attack chain is disturbingly simple: trick a developer into clicking a malicious link or opening a compromised project, and their credentials vanish into an attacker's possession. The implications cascade outward: compromised tokens grant access to private repositories, enable supply-chain poisoning, and create beachheads for lateral movement within organizations.

Microsoft has been notified, but the disclosure underscores a recurring nightmare for security-conscious teams: the most dangerous vulnerabilities often hide in plain sight, embedded in the workflow tools developers trust implicitly.

Hackers' Secret Weapon: Turning Nvidia VRAM Into Linux Swap SpaceshareCopied!

*A clever workaround transforms GPU memory into system swap—but the performance tradeoffs may cut deeper than the convenience gains*
▲ 334 pts  ·  88 comments

A GitHub project circulating among Linux enthusiasts this week offers a tantalizing solution to a familiar frustration: system RAM exhaustion. The nbd-vram utility leverages Nvidia GPUs as overflow swap space, treating VRAM as an extension of main memory when the kernel begins thrashing against physical limits. The technique is technically audacious—it treats GPU memory as a network block device, accessible via the Linux kernel's swap infrastructure.

The appeal is obvious for resource-constrained environments: a developer running memory-intensive workloads on a modest machine with a beefy GPU can suddenly address datasets that would otherwise demand hardware upgrades. However, the performance calculus is grim. VRAM access over the PCIe bus incurs latency penalties orders of magnitude larger than accessing system RAM; swap usage itself signals that the system has begun genuinely struggling. Combining the two creates a scenario where the machine buys time at the cost of responsiveness—a technical aspirin for a systemic headache.

The hack exemplifies the perpetual hacker mindset: if a resource exists and is underutilized, exploit it. Whether that exploitation serves practical needs or becomes a footnote in a resume project remains, for most users, an exercise in wishful thinking.

▲ 268 pts  ·  209 comments

A controlled study from Stanford Law School has delivered an uncomfortable verdict: large language models designed for legal reasoning now outperform tenured law professors across a range of standard legal tasks. The findings land at a moment when the legal profession is grappling with AI's asymmetric advantages—speed, consistency, tireless availability, and an ability to synthesize case law across centuries of precedent without fatigue or ego.

The research tested AI systems against experienced faculty members on tasks spanning contract analysis, case law synthesis, and legal memoranda drafting. The AI systems won with margins that suggest this is not a close competition. The implications cascade through law school economics, associate salary structures, and the fundamental value proposition of legal education itself. If a $20-per-month AI subscription can outperform a partner-track attorney billing $400 per hour, the incentive structure that has undergirded the legal profession for decades begins to fracture.

The legal establishment has historically resisted automation—bar associations guard entry, associate positions serve as credentialing gateways, and institutional inertia runs deep. But Stanford's findings may prove difficult to ignore. The question no longer centers on whether AI can do legal work competently; it centers on how an entire profession adapts when artificial intelligence becomes the better lawyer.

A Month With Clojure: One Developer's Reckoning With Functional Programming's Promises and PitfallsshareCopied!

*The LISP descendant delivered elegance and power—but asked harder questions about whether cleverness serves readability*
▲ 227 pts  ·  113 comments

A developer's candid reflection on a month-long immersion in Clojure surfaces the enduring tension between theoretical purity and practical productivity. The language—a modern LISP variant hosted on the JVM—promises the kind of cognitive clarity that object-oriented languages occlude beneath layers of state management and mutable abstractions. After four weeks, the developer's experience oscillates between moments of genuine elegance and extended stretches of bewilderment.

Clojure's strength lies precisely where its advocates claim: immutable data structures, functional composition, and the ability to express complex transformations with remarkable concision. A problem that might require defensive copying and state management in Java collapses into a few lines of Clojure. The experience is genuinely satisfying for certain categories of work—data transformation, concurrent processing, and algorithmic problem-solving. But the ecosystem remains thin compared to JavaScript or Python; debugging remains an exercise in deciphering cryptic stack traces; and the parentheses-heavy syntax, while internally consistent, demands a different cognitive model than most developers internalize.

The reflection points toward a quieter conclusion than Clojure's zealous advocates typically acknowledge: the language works brilliantly for specific problem domains, but remains a niche play for teams willing to absorb the learning curve and accept a smaller library ecosystem. For most working developers, the trade-off between expressiveness and ecosystem breadth simply doesn't justify the switch.
