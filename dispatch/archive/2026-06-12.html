When you ask for help without evidence of your own work, you're essentially asking someone to volunteer their scarcity to compensate for your abundance of laziness. Bedor's framing transforms this from a matter of etiquette into a matter of basic economics. The effort you demonstrate isn't politeness—it's proof that your request is worth the transaction cost.

The 273 comments reveal a profession wrestling with this tension at scale. Open source maintainers describe the exhaustion of fielding identical questions answered in documentation. Product managers discuss the noise-to-signal ratio of incoming requests. The essay functions as a mirror: it forces readers to audit their own asks and recognize moments when they've been the person demanding attention without investment.

The Invisible Labor That Saves Companies Millions: Why Prevention Gets No CreditshareCopied!

*A 25-year-old MIT paper resurfaces to explain why the engineers who stop disasters are thanked least of all*
▲ 490 pts  ·  165 comments

When Repenning and Sterman's paper circulated through Hacker News this week, it landed with the force of recognition—a diagnosis of a pathology as old as organizations themselves. The thesis is deceptively straightforward: people who prevent problems from occurring are systematically undervalued compared to people who solve problems after they've erupted. The engineer whose defensive infrastructure prevents an outage stays invisible; the engineer who heroically restores service becomes a hero. The structure of credit in organizations actively punishes prevention and rewards firefighting.

The implications are corrosive. Over time, organizations naturally drift toward crisis management because that's where recognition and promotion accumulate. Preventive work—code reviews, refactoring, monitoring infrastructure, capacity planning—is experienced as overhead, friction, busywork. The engineers performing it watch peers earn praise for dramatic saves while their methodical work earns only the absence of disaster. This creates perverse incentives: why spend months building robust systems when you could spend weeks responding to failures and earning career capital in the process?

The paper's 25-year-old analysis has metastasized into the present, where the comment section fills with recognition of how acutely this dynamic persists. SREs describe the invisible burden of keeping systems alive; security professionals discuss the thanklessness of undetected prevented breaches; architects lament that the elegant system nobody worries about generates zero visibility. The PDF circulates not as academic curiosity but as validation of a lived experience: the people preventing catastrophe are systematically erased from the narrative of organizational success.

*Simon Willison examines an AI assistant that redefines agency—and reveals both the promise and peril of proactive machines*
▲ 477 pts  ·  378 comments

Claude Fable, as analyzed through Simon Willison's characteristically acute lens, represents a category shift in how AI assistants relate to human intention. Rather than waiting passively for the next prompt, Fable exhibits what Willison describes as "relentless proactivity"—it identifies context, anticipates needs, and suggests actions before being asked. In principle, this sounds like the culmination of decades of AI interface design: a system that genuinely understands what you're trying to accomplish and smooths the path. In practice, it's considerably more unsettling.

Willison's analysis, which sparked 378 comments, centers on the tension between helpfulness and autonomy. When an AI system begins acting on inferred intent rather than explicit requests, it crosses a psychological threshold. The user's sense of control fragments. Is Fable actually understanding your goals, or is it hallucinating patterns and running with them? The risk isn't malice—it's misalignment at the level of inference. A system can be technically flawless and still deeply frustrating if its model of what you want diverges from what you actually want. The relentless proactivity, even when well-intentioned, can feel invasive.

Yet the 477 upvotes indicate genuine interest in this direction. Developers recognize that interface fatigue is real; constant prompting creates cognitive overhead. If machines could genuinely understand context and reduce back-and-forth friction, the value is enormous. The challenge lies in executing this without veering into paternalism—the sense that the system knows what you need better than you do. Willison's piece becomes essential reading precisely because it identifies this threshold and asks whether we're ready to cross it.
