The .self Domain Offers a Radical Answer to the Self-Hosting Question: Your Own TLDshareCopied!

*A new top-level domain experiment asks whether true digital sovereignty means escaping ICANN's jurisdiction entirely.*
▲ 534 pts  ·  310 comments

There's a philosophical fork happening in how technologists imagine the future of the internet. While most debates about decentralization revolve around blockchain or peer-to-peer protocols, a quieter proposal is gaining traction: what if you could own your own top-level domain? The .self initiative, championed by the Human-Centered Computing Foundation, proposes exactly that—a TLD designed explicitly to support self-hosting and personal infrastructure outside traditional DNS hierarchies.

The concept challenges a fundamental assumption of web architecture: that domain registration requires intermediaries. A .self domain would theoretically allow individuals and small collectives to register and manage identity at the top level, eliminating the need for domain registrars and ICANN gatekeeping. The 534-point reception suggests genuine interest, though the 310 comments hint at the technical and political complexity. Implementation would require consensus among resolver operators and recursive DNS servers—infrastructure controlled by major internet providers and governments. It's a bold proposal, but one that exposes how much of our "open" internet depends on centralized chokepoints.

If implemented, .self could function as a genuine alternative to the current regime, enabling people to publish content and host services under their own authority. Whether it succeeds depends entirely on whether enough infrastructure operators adopt it—a bootstrapping problem that mirrors every previous attempt to fork the internet's foundational layers.

Icon Liberation: Rogue Amoeba's Plea to Free Software From Design LicensingshareCopied!

*One developer's manifesto against the absurdity of icons locked behind proprietary licenses in an open-source ecosystem.*
▲ 499 pts  ·  146 comments

In what amounts to a pointed middle finger at the absurdity of modern software licensing, Rogue Amoeba has published a searing critique of icon licensing in the open-source ecosystem. The argument is deceptively simple: icons—small graphical symbols essential to usable software interfaces—are frequently locked behind commercial licenses, forcing open-source developers to either pay licensing fees, recreate the wheel, or ship uglier applications. "Free the Icons" isn't just a rallying cry; it's a technical and ethical indictment of a broken system.

The 499-point reception reflects a community frustrated but resigned to a silly situation. Icon designers have traditionally gatekept their work aggressively, operating under the assumption that pixel-perfect graphics represent significant intellectual property. But for small open-source projects and indie developers, commissioning icon design or licensing established libraries can represent prohibitive overhead. The result is a bizarre market failure where some of the world's most critical infrastructure—Linux utilities, data-processing tools, system daemons—ship with mediocre icons because adequate licensed assets remain economically inaccessible.

What makes this critique particularly sharp is its timeliness. As open-source software has become infrastructure rather than hobby, the friction between commercial design licensing and collaborative development has become undeniable. Rogue Amoeba's argument implicitly asks: why should design assets operate under different licensing paradigms than code itself? The answer, increasingly, is that they shouldn't.

One Million Passports Spill Across the Dark Web in Cannabis Club Operator BreachshareCopied!

*A massive credential dump highlights the baroque vulnerabilities lurking in niche software—and what happens when security gets outsourced to specialty vendors.*
▲ 293 pts  ·  178 comments

A million passport images and associated biometric data have materialized on underground forums, originating from Nefos PuffPal—software designed specifically for cannabis club management and member verification. The breach, documented by The Verge's security team, exposes a recurring nightmare of the internet age: critical identity infrastructure maintained by vendors most people have never heard of, operating under regulatory regimes that barely exist. The 293-point reception understates the severity—this is a category error in how we've architected identity verification.

The technical details reveal layers of negligence. PuffPal was tasked with verifying member identities—a requirement in jurisdictions where cannabis clubs operate in legal gray zones. Rather than implementing robust security protocols, the platform apparently stored passport images in plaintext-accessible databases with minimal encryption. When operators discovered the breach, they had no expedited disclosure process, no crisis infrastructure, and no established lines of communication with passport authorities. Victims had no notification mechanism. The data simply leaked into the world, awaiting exploitation by fraud networks.

What makes this particularly insidious is how it illustrates the second-order consequences of cannabis legalization's fragmented regulatory environment. Because cannabis remains federally illegal in the US and internationally uncoordinated, service providers operate in a compressed competitive environment where security budgets rank below customer acquisition. The result: critical identity documents are managed by vendors operating below the security standards we'd expect for online banking. This breach will likely catalyze demands for regulatory frameworks around identity verification in cannabis retail—a conversation that should have happened years ago.

In what might be the most philosophically dissident movement in recent open-source discourse, Open Source Low Tech has emerged as an explicit rejection of the compute-at-all-costs paradigm that's dominated development for the past decade. With 285 points and a notably sparse 60 comments, the movement seems to exist below the hype cycle's threshold, which may be precisely the point. The initiative advocates for engineering practices that prioritize efficiency, longevity, and minimal resource consumption—approaching software development as a materials science problem rather than an abstract engineering challenge.

The implications are subversive. Low-tech open source proposes that the most responsible software isn't the software written in Rust or optimized for cloud deployment—it's the software that works on constrained hardware, survives without constant updates, and doesn't demand server farms to deliver basic functionality. The philosophy draws from degrowth economics, right-to-repair movements, and historical software engineering practices from the 1980s, before abstractions became infinite and electricity seemed free. It's a direct challenge to the acceleration narrative that's shaped computing culture for two decades.

What's intellectually interesting is the timing. As energy costs rise, supply chains fracture, and the climate arithmetic of cryptocurrency-grade compute becomes undeniable, a movement arguing for methodical, efficient, minimal-resource software development reads less as nostalgic and more as pragmatic. The sparse comment count suggests most developers haven't yet confronted the question the initiative poses: what if the most advanced engineering isn't the most complex?
