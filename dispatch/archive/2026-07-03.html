Virginia Becomes First State to Ban the Sale of Location Data, Closing a Lucrative LoopholeshareCopied!

*The legislation marks a watershed moment in American privacy law, cutting off a $4 billion industry that has operated in the shadows*
▲ 818 pts  ·  128 comments

Virginia has enacted the nation's first outright ban on the sale of geolocation data, a sweeping move that effectively dismantles one of the tech industry's most insidious profit centers. The law, which takes effect in January 2026, prohibits companies from selling precise location information harvested from smartphones, vehicles, and connected devices—data that has become the lifeblood of surveillance capitalism, fueling everything from targeted advertising to predatory lending schemes. Unlike previous state privacy laws that offer opt-out mechanisms, Virginia's approach is categorical: no sale means no sale, regardless of consumer consent.

The legislation exposes a critical gap in the patchwork of American privacy regulation. While the FTC has cracked down on specific abuses, brokers have operated with near-total impunity, buying location signals from apps and mobile carriers, then packaging them for purchase by law enforcement, bail bondsmen, political campaigns, and data aggregators. A single smartphone generates thousands of location pings daily. Aggregated across millions of devices, this becomes a panopticon of unprecedented scale—yet consumers have had virtually no recourse.

Industry groups immediately signaled legal challenges, claiming the ban violates commercial free speech and disrupts legitimate business models. But Virginia's gambit may prove unstoppable. The law carries the weight of a state with significant corporate presence, and other legislatures are already watching. If the ban survives constitutional scrutiny, it could trigger a domino effect, forcing tech platforms and data brokers to fundamentally restructure their revenue models. For an industry built on invisible extraction, visibility—and accountability—is finally arriving.

Linux Kernel Security Bug Leaves Encrypted Disks Vulnerable to Memory AttacksshareCopied!

*A critical regression in LUKS suspend protection exposes disk encryption keys to forensic recovery since kernel 6.9*
▲ 483 pts  ·  209 comments

A serious security regression lurking in the Linux kernel since version 6.9 has been quietly exposing encrypted disk keys to memory-based attacks, a discovery that undermines a core assumption of full-disk encryption. When systems enter sleep mode, LUKS—the Linux Unified Key Setup standard for disk encryption—is supposed to wipe encryption keys from RAM to prevent attackers from performing cold-boot attacks or memory extraction. Somewhere between kernel versions, that critical safeguard broke. Keys now remain in memory during suspend, a flaw that turns any laptop or server into a potential forensic goldmine for sophisticated attackers with physical access.

The implications ripple outward. Organizations that assume encrypted disks provide protection against compromised hardware have been operating under a false premise. Law enforcement, intelligence agencies, and attackers with direct hardware access now have a multi-year window to extract keys from suspended systems. The vulnerability affects not just laptops but servers in data centers—any system where the kernel version wasn't carefully curated. Worse, the regression went unnoticed for months, suggesting the cryptographic community's testing regimen missed a fundamental check.

Linux maintainers have moved to patch the issue, but the damage lingers. Systems running kernels 6.9 through the present remain exposed unless updated, and there's no reliable way to know which deployments are vulnerable. This serves as a sharp reminder that encryption is only as strong as the systems protecting it—and kernel-level bugs can nullify years of cryptographic work in a single regression.

The Apple CarPlay Debate Gets Heated: Is Integration Feature or Trap?shareCopied!

*Tech critics clash over whether Apple's vehicle integration is a consumer convenience or a walled-garden power play*
▲ 328 pts  ·  435 comments

Casey Liss's provocative framing of Apple CarPlay as "additive" rather than transformative has ignited a fierce debate within the tech community about what integration actually means—and who benefits from it. The argument cuts to the heart of platform strategy: CarPlay appears to enhance the driving experience by seamlessly mirroring your iPhone's capabilities onto vehicle displays. But this framing obscures a deeper reality: Apple is inserting itself as an intermediary between drivers and their cars, capturing data and controlling the user experience in a space where safety and autonomy should reign supreme.

Liss's critics counter that calling CarPlay merely "additive" whitewashes Apple's actual ambitions. The ecosystem isn't optional—it's become the default experience for iPhone users, subtly discouraging aftermarket solutions and car-native interfaces. Apple controls which apps appear, how navigation works, and what data gets shared. For drivers, this feels seamless. For independence-minded users and automakers, it's a concerning precedent where a smartphone company wields disproportionate influence over vehicle interfaces. The distinction between "adding value" and "capturing a market" matters, especially when the market controls how people move through the world.

What's at stake is the principle of interoperability in an increasingly connected world. If Apple's vision prevails—where CarPlay becomes the de facto standard for vehicle integration—we've accepted a future where a single company mediates the relationship between drivers and their machines. That's not additive. It's consolidation, dressed in the language of convenience.

Scott Aaronson Sounds Alarm on America's Location Data CrisisshareCopied!

*The MIT theorist argues that the geolocation surveillance state has become an existential threat to political freedom*
▲ 320 pts  ·  96 comments

Theoretical computer scientist Scott Aaronson has waded into the privacy debate with an uncharacteristically urgent essay titled "An American Privacy Emergency," arguing that the nation's unregulated location data ecosystem now poses a direct threat to democratic function. His thesis is stark: when governments and political operatives can track citizens' movements with near-perfect fidelity, the psychological preconditions for dissent collapse. People self-censor not because surveillance is hypothetically possible, but because it's continuous and invisible. The panopticon isn't coming; it's already here.

Aaronson's contribution to this well-worn debate lies in his specificity. He documents how law enforcement has weaponized location data against protesters, how campaigns have used geolocation to identify and micro-target swing voters, and how the infrastructure of tracking is so distributed that no single regulation can close it. Data brokers, cellular carriers, app makers, and government agencies form a Byzantine system where accountability evaporates. Even Virginia's incoming ban on location data sales won't solve the problem because government agencies have other pathways to the same information. The system is built to be Hydra-headed.

What makes Aaronson's intervention matter is his credibility as a technologist. When computer scientists—people who understand the architecture of surveillance intimately—start talking like civil rights activists, the message carries weight. His conclusion is radical for the discourse: incremental regulation won't work. Only structural change—requiring affirmative consent before any location tracking, severe penalties for abuse, and genuine data minimization—can restore privacy to a meaningful concept. The current trajectory, he argues, leads toward a society where freedom exists only where cameras aren't pointed.
