Europe's Chat Control Law Clears Parliament, Handing Governments Mass Surveillance PowersshareCopied!

*The EU votes to scan private messages for abuse material, igniting fierce debate over encryption and digital rights*
▲ 1422 pts  ·  678 comments

The European Parliament has voted to advance Chat Control 1.0, legislation that would mandate messaging platforms scan private communications for child sexual abuse material—a move that marks one of the continent's most aggressive regulatory swings toward surveillance. The measure, which still faces final procedural hurdles, would require end-to-end encrypted services to deploy detection technology on user conversations, effectively creating a backdoor into systems designed to be impenetrable.

Privacy advocates and security researchers have savaged the proposal as fundamentally incompatible with robust encryption. The technology required to scan encrypted messages without decrypting them wholesale doesn't yet exist at the scale demanded, critics argue, forcing platforms to either weaken encryption or implement detection at the expense of user privacy. German MEP Patrick Breyer, who has become the law's most vocal opponent, warns the framework sacrifices the digital rights of all users—including children—in pursuit of a security measure that experts say will prove ineffective against sophisticated criminals.

The vote reveals a deepening philosophical rift between American tech companies and European regulators over whose responsibility it is to police online harm. Brussels is betting that legal mandates can work where voluntary cooperation has faltered, but the precedent it sets—allowing governments to demand surveillance capabilities in exchange for market access—could reshape the entire landscape of digital communication globally.

Users must express complete thoughts, stories, or arguments in precisely 18 words—no more, no fewer. The constraint has proven far more generative than its stark simplicity suggests, spawning thousands of submissions that range from micro-fiction to political commentary to philosophical musings.

The project taps into a growing backlash against algorithmic feeds and engagement metrics that reward verbosity over precision. Writers report the limitation forces them to strip away jargon, redundancy, and rhetorical padding, leaving only marrow. The result is often startling: intimate narratives, sharp observations, and moments of unexpected profundity compressed into a haiku-adjacent form.

Whether 18 Words becomes a sustained platform or a flash of internet culture remains to be seen. But its rapid adoption suggests audiences are hungry for digital spaces that reward thoughtfulness over volume—a quiet counter-signal in an age of algorithmic excess.

Postgres Gets a Rust Rewrite That Passes Every Single Regression TestshareCopied!

*A solo developer rebuilds the legendary database engine in Rust, signaling a potential shift in database infrastructure*
▲ 692 pts  ·  581 comments

A striking milestone has emerged from the database community: a Rust rewrite of PostgreSQL, the venerable open-source relational database that powers countless critical systems, has now passed 100% of Postgres's regression test suite. The achievement, documented on GitHub under pgrust, represents not merely a technical accomplishment but a philosophical statement: even foundational systems written in C can be reimagined in memory-safe languages without sacrificing compatibility or performance.

Postgres has been written in C since its inception, a choice that made sense in the 1980s and 1990s when memory safety wasn't a priority and C's raw speed was essential. But decades of security patches, buffer overflows, and use-after-free vulnerabilities have exposed the costs of that decision. A Rust rewrite doesn't automatically solve every problem—logic bugs and architectural issues can exist in any language—but it eliminates an entire class of vulnerabilities that have plagued database systems since their inception. The creator has managed to achieve feature parity with the original while maintaining the test compatibility that enterprises demand.

Whether pgrust becomes the foundation of a new Postgres distribution or remains a proof-of-concept, its existence reframes a crucial debate in systems programming: legacy doesn't mean untouchable. If PostgreSQL—arguably the most important open-source database in the world—can be rewritten in Rust without breaking its contract, what other foundational infrastructure should be reconsidered?
