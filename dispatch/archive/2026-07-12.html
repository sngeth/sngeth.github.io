▲ 201 pts  ·  206 comments

The minimalist interior design movement—sparse furniture, empty walls, muted palettes—has conquered affluent homes and corporate offices over the past decade. But a new study from researchers studying cognitive load suggests this aesthetic trend carries a hidden psychological cost. Minimalist environments may actually strain working memory and increase mental fatigue, countering the intuitive appeal of "less is more."

The mechanism is unexpected: visual simplicity doesn't necessarily reduce cognitive demand. Instead, sparse environments force viewers to fill gaps with interpretation and assumption. Absent visual structure, the brain works harder to extract meaning. When an office wall is blank, your eye doesn't rest—it searches. When decorative hierarchy is absent, decisions about where to focus become effortful. The study's findings directly challenge the design orthodoxy of the last fifteen years, suggesting that some visual complexity may actually aid cognition rather than hinder it.
