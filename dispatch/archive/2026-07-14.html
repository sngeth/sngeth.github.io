California is preparing to legislate against one of the internet's most insidious design patterns: infinite scroll. Meta, TikTok, and YouTube have engineered these systems with the precision of Las Vegas casinos, optimizing for maximum engagement through psychological principles borrowed from addiction research. The California bill doesn't merely target a UX pattern; it attacks the mathematical machinery underpinning platform profitability.

The industry's fierce resistance signals how existential this threat actually is. For Meta especially, which has staked its entire business model on bottomless content consumption, the prospect of algorithmic friction represents an existential challenge. Defenders argue the bill is unworkable—how do you technically enforce such restrictions? Critics counter that the impossibility of perfect enforcement shouldn't prevent harm reduction. The real question isn't whether California's law will succeed, but whether its passage signals a broader regulatory appetite to dismantle the attention-maximization arms race that has colonized digital life.

Stanford's 'Fundamentals of Wireless Communication' Remains the Canonical Text Two Decades LatershareCopied!

*A freely available 2005 textbook continues to shape how engineers think about signal propagation and network design*
▲ 138 pts  ·  6 comments

Rarity in academic publishing: a textbook that improves with age. Two decades after publication, Stanford's "Fundamentals of Wireless Communication" persists as the indispensable reference for anyone serious about understanding radio propagation, channel modeling, and the physical layer constraints that govern every wireless system. That it remains freely available online only amplifies its influence.

The text's longevity stems from its foundational rigor. Rather than chasing technological trends, the authors excavated first principles—the mathematics of fading channels, the Shannon capacity limits that constrain all wireless systems, the geometry of signal propagation. These fundamentals haven't aged because they're not about specific technologies; they're about physical reality. A 5G engineer grappling with millimeter-wave propagation returns to the same channel models; an IoT developer optimizing for range encounters identical tradeoffs that the text explores.

In an era of proliferating online courses and YouTube tutorials, this text represents something increasingly rare: a work of sufficient intellectual depth that re-reading it yields new insights with each encounter. For practitioners, it's not supplementary reading—it's the standard against which all subsequent wireless communications education is measured. Its continued availability as a free resource, rare for such authoritative work, has made it arguably more influential than if it remained gated behind publisher paywalls.
