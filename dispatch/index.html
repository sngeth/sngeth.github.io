Texas Instruments Resurrects the Graphing Calculator for the AI AgeshareCopied!

*The TI-84 Evo promises faster processors and modern connectivity—but can nostalgia overcome the smartphone revolution?*
▲ 464 pts  ·  394 comments

For four decades, the TI-84 has been the de facto standard in American high schools, a chunky black rectangle that survived every technological upheaval from the internet to the smartphone. Now Texas Instruments is betting that educators and students still want dedicated hardware. The new TI-84 Evo, unveiled this week, represents the company's most significant refresh in years—faster silicon, enhanced graphing capabilities, and wireless connectivity that finally acknowledges the 21st century.

The move is audacious precisely because it swims against the current. Graphing applications on tablets and computers have matured considerably. GeoGebra, Desmos, and other free or low-cost alternatives have democratized mathematical visualization. Yet TI's installed base remains formidable: millions of standardized tests still permit only official calculators, and teacher inertia runs deep. The Evo doesn't replace the original; it runs its firmware, preserving backward compatibility with two generations of curricula.

What's striking is the company's implicit acknowledgment that the calculator market has bifurcated. Professional mathematicians and engineers abandoned graphing calculators long ago. But in K-12 education—where standardized testing regimes create artificial demand—TI retains something closer to a monopoly. The Evo isn't a product born from innovation pressure; it's a product born from an absence of disruptive pressure.

For four decades, the TI-84 has been the de facto standard in American high schools—and TI is betting it still matters.

Scientists Discover the Dream as a Hidden Classroom—Should We Be Learning in Our Sleep?shareCopied!

*New neuroscience research reveals we can acquire and refine skills while unconscious—raising ethical questions about whose dreams we own.*
▲ 351 pts  ·  201 comments

The fantasy of learning while asleep has captivated researchers and sci-fi writers for generations. This week, fresh research published in peer-reviewed journals suggests that the fantasy might be closer to reality than previously thought. During REM sleep, when dreams are most vivid, the brain appears capable of not just consolidating existing memories but actually acquiring new information and practicing motor skills—without a conscious mind present to experience it.

The implications ripple across multiple domains. Athletes could theoretically optimize muscle memory during sleep. Musicians might internalize complex passages while dreaming. Students cramming for exams might find relief in a good night's rest that's doing more cognitive work than we ever suspected. Yet the research raises unsettling questions about neurological autonomy and consent. If knowledge and skills can be implanted during sleep—if the brain can learn without the self's permission—what becomes of the line between self-improvement and violation?

Neuroscientists emphasize the research remains preliminary; translation from lab findings to scalable, ethical application is years away. But the momentum is unmistakable. Silicon Valley, always eager to extract productivity from every waking (and now sleeping) moment, is already circling. The question is no longer whether we *can* learn while dreaming, but whether we *should*—and who gets to decide.

Ask.com Dies Quietly, Taking a Search Engine Legacy Into the Digital GraveshareCopied!

*Once a direct challenger to Google, the portal has finally been shuttered—marking the end of an era when search was still contested territory.*
▲ 284 pts  ·  146 comments

Ask.com, the natural-language search engine that once promised to overthrow Google's tyranny, has finally closed its doors. The announcement came without fanfare this week—no blog post, no retrospective, just a landing page and the sound of digital silence. For those who remember the late 1990s and early 2000s, when search was still a battleground and upstarts believed they could dethrone the Mountain View giant, Ask.com's death marks the end of an era.

Launched in 1997 as Ask Jeeves, the platform differentiated itself on a simple premise: users should be able to ask questions in natural language rather than constructing Boolean queries for machines. The anthropomorphic butler mascot—a nod to P.G. Wodehouse's fictional valet—gave the service personality at a moment when the web still felt playful. For a brief window in the 2000s, Ask held meaningful market share. But Google's relentless optimization and algorithmic dominance proved insurmountable. Ask became a cautionary tale: in winner-take-most markets, being second-best is not a viable business.

The closure is less about Ask.com's failure than about the crystallization of Google's monopoly. Search consolidation happened so gradually that few noticed when competition effectively ended. Ask.com's death is a tombstone marking that transition—a reminder that the open web once had contenders, and now has a crowned champion.

Noctua's Confession: Why the PC Cooling Market Struggles With Simple ColorshareCopied!

*The premium fan manufacturer reveals the absurd supply-chain and market-segmentation hurdles behind a trivial product variation.*
▲ 275 pts  ·  117 comments

Noctua, the Austrian cooling specialist revered for whisper-quiet fans and obsessive engineering, has published a remarkably candid explanation of a consumer irritation: why does it take the company years to release black versions of its products? The answer exposes the brittle complexity lurking beneath hardware manufacturing—and why even boutique brands operate under suffocating constraints.

The core issue isn't engineering difficulty; it's market segmentation and supply-chain mathematics. Noctua's signature brown-and-beige aesthetic is intentional branding, not accident. Releasing black variants requires separate tooling, different plastic compounds, additional SKUs, and parallel inventory management. For a company producing fans in limited volumes—however premium—the overhead of a color variant is substantial. Retailers demand minimum orders; manufacturing facilities must dedicate production runs; logistics networks multiply in complexity. What seems trivial to a consumer asking "why not just use different plastic?" requires Noctua to justify the costs against projected demand.

More provocatively, Noctua's post reveals how PC enthusiasts—a historically underserved market—have been trained into patience. A community that once felt forgotten by major manufacturers now accepts years-long delays for minor variations on premium products. The blog post is diplomatic, but the subtext is clear: we could do this faster, but you haven't convinced us the market is large enough to justify it. In hardware, the inverse of Moore's Law appears to apply: complexity scales faster than capability.
