The Vulnerability Report Is Dead. Long Live Responsible Disclosure.shareCopied!

*As zero-days proliferate, the old protocol of coordinated disclosure faces an existential reckoning.*
▲ 293 pts  ·  159 comments

The ritual of the responsible vulnerability disclosure—researcher finds bug, notifies vendor in secret, waits 90 days, publishes findings with fanfare—has become a ghost of its former utility. Filippo Valsorda, a cryptography engineer at Cloudflare, argues in a sprawling technical essay that the entire framework has calcified into irrelevance, gutted by the acceleration of both attack and defense cycles. When nation-states can weaponize exploits faster than patches deploy, and when entire supply chains crumble in weeks, the idea that a three-month embargo serves anyone but the vendor feels quaint.

Valsorda doesn't call for disclosure's abolition so much as its obsolescence. The real problem, he suggests, isn't that researchers are leaking vulnerabilities to the highest bidder—it's that the baseline assumptions protecting vendors no longer hold. Zero-day brokers, security researchers, and threat actors now operate in a market where information has half-life measured in days. Coordinated disclosure assumes scarcity and control; the internet assumes abundance and chaos. The mismatch has created perverse incentives: researchers delay publication to avoid weaponization, vendors delay patches to avoid liability, and the ecosystem collectively fails to address systemic weaknesses until catastrophe strikes.

The essay resonates because it names a discomfort the industry has been skirting for years. If vulnerability reports aren't moving the needle on security—if patches languish undeployed, if zero-days still proliferate despite responsible practices—then perhaps the problem isn't the protocol. Perhaps it's the model itself.

Apple Absorbs Swift Package Index, Signaling Long-Term Commitment to Developer InfrastructureshareCopied!

*The acquisition marks a quiet but significant shift: Big Tech finally owning the pipes it builds on.*
▲ 209 pts  ·  69 comments

For years, the Swift Package Index operated as a vital but precarious commons—a third-party registry indexing and surfacing packages for Apple's programming language, maintained by a skeleton crew of volunteers and light sponsorship. On Tuesday, Apple announced it would acquire the index wholesale, folding it into its official developer infrastructure and guaranteeing funding and permanence for a tool that had become essential plumbing for the Swift ecosystem.

The move reflects a maturation in how platform companies approach open-source governance. Rather than hoarding package management or starving external alternatives into irrelevance, Apple is recognizing that a thriving package ecosystem benefits everyone—including Apple. The Swift Package Index, unlike npm or PyPI, had always been community-first; its incorporation into Apple proper removes uncertainty about its long-term viability while preserving the collaborative ethos that built it. The index's maintainers will now work directly with Apple's language and tools teams, theoretically accelerating integration with Xcode and reducing friction for developers.

Yet the acquisition also signals something less optimistic: the consolidation of developer infrastructure under corporate control. When package discovery, versioning, and distribution live entirely within a vendor's ecosystem, the boundaries between platform and monopoly blur. Apple's track record of respecting developer autonomy is mixed. The open question isn't whether the index will be well-maintained—it almost certainly will be. It's whether independence itself has become a luxury no open-source project can afford.

Rhombus Language Reaches 1.0: Racket's Answer to Modern Syntax PragmatismshareCopied!

*After years of iteration, the Lisp dialect finally closes the syntax gap without abandoning its philosophical roots.*
▲ 177 pts  ·  48 comments

Rhombus, the pragmatic new face of Racket, has reached version 1.0—and in doing so, answers a question the Lisp community has been dodging for decades: can you keep the power while ditching the parentheses? Built atop Racket's proven macro system and runtime, Rhombus introduces a syntax that looks vaguely conventional—infix operators, statement-like forms, fewer nested lists—without sacrificing the metaprogramming capabilities that made Racket distinct.

The language represents a conscious bet that syntax matters more than purists admit. Racket had amassed a loyal but marginal following; developers respected its technical elegance but often chose Python or JavaScript for practical work. Rhombus doesn't abandon Racket's core—it wraps it in ergonomics. Pattern matching, algebraic data types, and first-class macros remain; they're just written in a way that doesn't require emacs muscle memory to parse. The 1.0 milestone signals that the implementation is stable enough for real projects, moving Rhombus from experimental curiosity to viable alternative.

What's notable is the ecosystem positioning. Rhombus isn't trying to displace Python for data science or Node for web development. It's targeting the niche Racket always occupied: domains where expressiveness and control matter more than library abundance. For that audience—educational tools, domain-specific languages, systems requiring deep customization—Rhombus may finally offer what they've been waiting for: Racket's power with mainstream syntax sensibility.

Infrastructure · Cloud

Bunny Flips DNS to Free: The Race for Infrastructure Becomes a Race to the BottomshareCopied!

*A CDN provider's giveaway of DNS services hints at a brutal reckoning in edge computing economics.*
▲ 170 pts  ·  48 comments

Bunny, a European CDN provider and scrappy alternative to Cloudflare and Akamai, announced it would offer DNS services for free—not as a loss leader for premium features, but as a straightforward public good. The move is generous on its surface and ruthless in its implication: Bunny is so confident in its cost structure that it can monetize only the services where margins haven't yet collapsed to zero.

DNS resolution was once a revenue driver; now it's become a commodity so commodified that free is competitive. Bunny's calculus is simple: if DNS is free everywhere else (Google, Cloudflare, Amazon all offer it), charging for it is friction. Better to absorb the cost, build goodwill, and capture users who will eventually upgrade to the services where Bunny can actually extract value. The economics tell the real story: DNS queries are cheap to serve at scale, and the marginal cost of one more customer is negligible. When infrastructure becomes that abundant, giveaways aren't altruism—they're standard business practice.

But the broader pattern should concern anyone watching cloud economics. Every major provider in CDN and DNS is engaged in a relentless commoditization spiral. Services that once commanded margins now approach zero; differentiation bleeds into performance minutiae and feature coverage. Bunny's move is rational, even admirable—it's also a sign that the moat around edge infrastructure is eroding. When providers start competing by making foundational services free, it suggests the consolidation phase has ended and the efficiency phase has begun. The survivors will be those who can operate at near-zero margin and still extract value from volume or from services upstream.
