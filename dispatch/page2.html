GPU Sharing Gets a Democratic Upgrade: sllm Lets Developers Split Hardware Without the Vendor LockdownshareCopied!

*A new platform promises unlimited token generation and collaborative compute at scale—but the real story is its challenge to the cloud oligarchy's token-metering model*
▲ 162 pts  ·  79 comments

sllm launches into the Hacker News conversation with a pointed value proposition: split a GPU node with other developers and consume tokens without artificial caps. With 162 points and 79 comments (a notably robust discussion ratio), the platform is tapping into real friction in the AI development workflow—the economics of inference, the overhead of compute allocation, and the growing sense that GPU access shouldn't be gatekept by margin-conscious cloud providers.

The mechanics are elegant on paper. Instead of spinning up isolated inference instances and paying per million tokens, developers share a single GPU node. The cost structure flattens. Latency becomes a shared problem, not an individual burden. For teams running moderate-scale LLM workloads or experimenting with multiple models in parallel, this represents genuine savings and flexibility. The "unlimited tokens" framing—aggressive as it is—signals a deliberate pivot away from the token-metering model that OpenAI and Anthropic have spent years perfecting and monetizing.

The comment thread reveals both enthusiasm and skepticism: technical questions about scheduling, security isolation, and fair-use policies against a backdrop of genuine demand. If sllm can prove that collaborative GPU access works reliably and securely, it threatens the margin assumptions baked into every cloud provider's inference pricing. If it doesn't, it becomes a cautionary tale about infrastructure economics. Either way, the signal is clear—developers are actively looking for an exit from the token meter.

The Indie Internet Index Wants to Rescue the Web From Algorithmic Monotony—By Crowdsourcing ItshareCopied!

*A grassroots effort to catalog forgotten corners of the web surfaces amid growing fatigue with algorithmic feeds and corporate walled gardens*
▲ 151 pts  ·  29 comments

The Indie Internet Index arrives as a quiet rebellion against the algorithmic feed, asking Hacker News users to submit their favorite independent websites and help build a living directory of human-curated corners of the web. With 151 points and 29 comments, it's capturing appetite for an alternative indexing logic—not engagement metrics, not click-through rates, but genuine human preference.

The premise is deceptively simple: crowdsource a map of the internet that *isn't* controlled by SEO optimizers or attention-capture machinery. In an era when most web navigation flows through five or six platforms, the very idea of a "forgotten website" feels quaint and slightly radical. Yet the participation suggests nostalgia isn't the only driver. Developers and curious web citizens seem genuinely interested in discovery mechanics that aren't surveillance-powered, feeds that aren't personalised into silos, and collections that trust human judgment over behavioral signals.

What remains to be seen is whether an index—no matter how well-intentioned—can survive at scale. Curation demands effort. Spam will arrive. The original web directories (Yahoo, DMOZ) didn't fail because the concept was flawed; they failed because they couldn't compete with algorithms. But this moment, with its explicit anti-algorithm stance and indie aesthetic, suggests the appetite for an alternative may finally be strong enough to sustain it.

Zero-Power PCB Badges Hack Their Way Into Hardware Culture—Without Batteries or ExcusesshareCopied!

*An open-source hackathon badge design that requires no power source is redefining what's possible at the intersection of minimalism and electronics*
▲ 110 pts  ·  10 comments

Kai Pereira has published an open-source design for PCB hackathon badges that operate without batteries, without power supplies, and without apology—achieving 110 points and 10 comments that lean heavily technical and genuinely impressed. Overglade Badges represent a specific design philosophy: maximum functionality extracted from minimum infrastructure, which, in the world of conference swag and maker culture, reads as almost heretical.

The innovation sits at the intersection of constraint and creativity. Traditional hackathon badges are either powered devices requiring battery management (and eventual e-waste) or static, printed novelties. Overglade Badges operate in a third mode: low-power or passive electronics that derive functionality from proximity, interaction, or environmental stimulus. The GitHub repository suggests genuine engineering depth—PCB layouts, component specifications, documentation intended for others to build and modify.

The modest comment count belies the work's significance within maker circles. Hardware engineers, conference organisers, and sustainability-minded builders are precisely the audience who recognise what zero-power constraints demand: rethinking every component, every circuit, every assumption about what a "useful" device looks like. Open-sourcing the design means Overglade Badges can evolve across hackathons, conferences, and workshops—each iteration teaching the next builder something new about doing more with infinitely less.
