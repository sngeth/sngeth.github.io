Popular Note-Taking Plugin Weaponized to Deliver Phantom Pulse Remote Access TrojanshareCopied!

*Security researchers uncover sophisticated supply-chain attack targeting Obsidian users through a trusted ecosystem plugin*
▲ 228 pts  ·  116 comments

Obsidian, the minimalist note-taking application beloved by developers and researchers, has become an unexpected vector for sophisticated malware deployment. Security analysts at NetSecOps discovered that threat actors compromised or created a malicious plugin within Obsidian's plugin ecosystem, using it as a delivery mechanism for Phantom Pulse, a modular remote access trojan capable of establishing persistent control over victim systems. The attack exploits the fundamental trust users place in community-driven plugin repositories—a vulnerability that has become increasingly common as open ecosystems scale.

The Phantom Pulse RAT, once installed through the weaponized plugin, grants attackers sweeping capabilities: keystroke logging, file exfiltration, command execution, and lateral movement across networks. What makes this campaign particularly insidious is its targeting precision; researchers suggest the attackers focused on technical professionals—security researchers, developers, and IT administrators—whose compromised systems could serve as beachheads for broader organizational breaches. The plugin's integration into Obsidian's native architecture meant the malware operated with the same permissions and system access as the legitimate application itself.

The incident underscores a critical blind spot in software supply chain security: the trustworthiness of plugin ecosystems. Unlike centralized app stores with rigorous vetting, community plugin repositories often rely on user reporting and retroactive detection. Obsidian maintainers have since removed the malicious plugin and released mitigation guidance, but the breach has already exposed the scalability of social engineering attacks that exploit developer trust.

The Perfect Frame: How James Burke Engineered Television's Most Haunting Single ShotshareCopied!

*In an age of rapid cuts and visual cacophony, one moment demonstrates why cinema's greatest power lies in patient observation*
▲ 192 pts  ·  83 comments

Television rarely permits itself the luxury of stillness. In an era of fractured attention and algorithmic pacing, extended single shots have become almost subversive—an act of defiance against the medium's own restlessness. Yet filmmaker James Burke achieved something rarer still: a single, meticulously composed frame that transcended technical mastery to become a statement about human vulnerability, institutional power, and the unseen violence embedded in ordinary moments.

Burke's shot, preserved and celebrated among cinematographers as a masterclass in visual storytelling, works precisely because it refuses to dramatize. The frame contains no overt action, no musical crescendo, no manipulative editing. Instead, it positions the viewer as an unwilling witness to a quieter kind of devastation—the kind that unfolds in the space between words, in the geometry of bodies occupying institutional space, in light that reveals rather than conceals. The composition forces viewers to do the interpretive labor themselves, making the moment not a statement Burke makes *for* us, but a question he poses *to* us.

In retrospect, Burke's shot represents a counterargument to the assumption that television's power lies in acceleration and novelty. His was a bet that audiences would sit with discomfort, that a single, perfectly calibrated image could generate more emotional resonance than a sequence of dramatic beats. Two decades later, that bet appears vindicated—the shot remains discussed, studied, and replicated precisely because it understands something fundamental about the human eye: we don't look away from what's true.

Open Source · Security

Curl Vulnerability Discovered: The Open-Source Library That Powers the Internet Faces New Security TestshareCopied!

*A fresh flaw in the ubiquitous data-transfer tool reveals why even the most audited software remains a constant frontier*
▲ 188 pts  ·  76 comments

Curl occupies a peculiar position in the technology hierarchy—so foundational that its absence would break the internet, yet sufficiently unglamorous that most developers rarely contemplate its existence. When Daniel Haxx, curl's creator and maintainer, announced a newly discovered vulnerability, it served as another reminder that security theater cannot exist in systems this critical. The flaw, identified by the Mythos security project, joins a decades-long roster of vulnerabilities in a tool that has become as invisible and essential as TCP/IP itself.

What makes curl vulnerabilities particularly consequential is their reach: the library is embedded in countless applications, from command-line tools to enterprise software to embedded systems in devices most people never think about. A single flaw in curl's data-handling logic potentially cascades across millions of installations globally. Yet the discovery process remains painstakingly manual. Mythos's identification of this particular vulnerability reflects an ongoing reality: despite sophisticated fuzzing tools, formal verification methods, and decades of security research, humans still discover critical flaws through patterns of observation and experimentation. Automation remains effective at finding certain classes of bugs; the harder problems still require intuition and adversarial thinking.

The incident encapsulates a broader paradox in open-source security. Curl is simultaneously over-audited and under-resourced—reviewed by security researchers constantly, yet maintained by a tiny team operating on gratitude and caffeine. The vulnerability will likely be patched swiftly, and the cycle will repeat. But each discovery reinforces an uncomfortable truth: there is no endpoint to security, no moment where critical software becomes invulnerable. There is only the perpetual work of finding problems before adversaries do.
