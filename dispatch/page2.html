Linux's I/O Revolution: Why io_uring Is Quietly Eating epoll's LunchshareCopied!

*A technical deep-dive shows how a newer Linux subsystem outperforms the two-decade standard in ways that matter for real systems*
▲ 174 pts  ·  42 comments

For twenty years, epoll has been the gold standard for asynchronous I/O on Linux. Web servers, databases, and message queues have built their concurrency models around it. But io_uring, introduced in Linux 5.1, represents a fundamental rethinking of how applications and the kernel should coordinate on I/O operations—and the technical advantages are substantial enough that the ecosystem is quietly beginning a migration.

Epoll's architectural limitation stems from its polling model: applications must ask the kernel "has anything happened?" in a loop, creating inherent latency and context-switch overhead. io_uring inverts this paradigm through shared ring buffers and completion queues, allowing applications to submit batches of I/O requests and receive batches of results without system call overhead. The difference isn't academic—high-throughput systems see measurable reductions in CPU usage and latency tail percentiles. The tradeoff is complexity: io_uring's API demands more from developers, and the learning curve is steep.

The 174-point ranking and 42 comments suggest this resonates primarily with systems engineers and performance-obsessed developers rather than the broader community. The comparative analysis matters because it signals what the industry is optimizing for: not backward compatibility or ease of use, but extracting maximum throughput from modern hardware. For Linux kernel contributors and infrastructure companies, io_uring isn't a future option—it's the present.

*A coordinated breach of telecommunications infrastructure exploits the civic trust embedded in emergency warning systems*
▲ 136 pts  ·  100 comments

An unauthorized alert suddenly flooded cell phones across Brazil, marking a disturbing escalation in how adversaries target critical infrastructure. Rather than attacking banking systems or stealing data, the hackers weaponized something more fundamental: the emergency alert infrastructure that Brazilians instinctively trust. The fake alert created panic, confusion, and—most dangerously—eroded public confidence in legitimate warnings when genuine emergencies occur.

The technical intrusion likely exploited gaps in authentication or access controls within telecommunications operators' networks, though the exact vector remains under investigation. What's significant is the tactic's effectiveness: emergency alert systems succeed precisely because they bypass users' normal skepticism. When a cell phone blares with an official warning, people act first and verify later. Hijacking this channel for a test message or hoax represents a form of infrastructure terrorism that requires no technical sophistication from end-users to propagate.

The 100 comments on the story reflect justified alarm. Brazilian authorities and global telecom regulators now face the unsexy but critical work of hardening these systems: better authentication protocols, audit logging, access segmentation. This breach exposes a vulnerability that exists across every nation with modern emergency alert capabilities. Until telecommunications operators treat alert systems with the same security rigor as financial networks, state actors and sophisticated criminals will continue finding these systems irresistible targets.
