ABC News Quietly Dismantles FiveThirtyEight Archive in Controversial PurgeshareCopied!

*The decision to erase years of data journalism raises questions about corporate stewardship of digital media*
▲ 218 pts  ·  101 comments

ABC News has systematically removed all FiveThirtyEight articles from public view, effectively erasing one of the internet's most influential data journalism operations from its institutional home. The move—discovered by observers who found previously indexed pages now inaccessible—represents a stark departure from how legacy media companies typically handle acquired properties, even when divesting from them.

FiveThirtyEight, Nate Silver's brainchild that revolutionized election forecasting and statistical analysis in journalism, became part of Disney's ABC News in 2018. The platform produced thousands of rigorous analyses spanning politics, sports, science, and culture. Its disappearance suggests either a technical migration gone wrong or a deliberate decision to de-platform the content—neither explanation settles well with archivists and media scholars who view such purges as digital vandalism.

The incident reignites a simmering debate about who truly owns digital journalism when corporations acquire media properties. Readers and researchers lost access to years of methodologically sound reporting without warning or alternative hosting. For an industry already fragile in the eyes of the public, the casual deletion of credible work is another erosion of trust in institutional gatekeeping.

*The open-source team chat platform moves to protect its future through formal nonprofit governance*
▲ 194 pts  ·  50 comments

Zulip, the open-source team communication platform known for its threading model and institutional-grade features, has formally established a foundation to ensure its long-term survival independent of commercial pressures. The move signals growing concern among open-source projects about relying on venture funding or corporate benevolence, particularly as the software industry tightens.

The foundation structure grants Zulip legal autonomy and community governance mechanisms that resist the kind of sudden pivots or abandonment that plague projects dependent on single-company patronage. With transparent operations and democratic decision-making, Zulip positions itself as a counterweight to proprietary chat dominance while avoiding the venture-capital trap that often compromises open-source values. The approach reflects maturation: successful projects increasingly recognize that sustainability requires institutional scaffolding, not just technical merit.

For organizations evaluating communication infrastructure, the foundation model offers reassurance—Zulip won't vanish if funding dries up or a parent company shifts strategy. This institutional commitment matters in an era where teams can no longer afford to adopt tools that might evaporate, making Zulip's move both a principled stance and a practical competitive advantage.

Waymo's Fleet Stumbles Through Puddles, Exposing Blind Spot in Autonomous DrivingshareCopied!

*A recall of 3,800 robotaxis reveals that wet roads remain a genuine hazard for self-driving cars*
▲ 147 pts  ·  138 comments

Waymo has initiated a software update across 3,800 robotaxis after discovering they repeatedly drove into standing water—a seemingly elementary environmental hazard that exposes a lingering vulnerability in machine perception. The vehicles misidentified or ignored bodies of water on roadways, treating them as navigable terrain rather than obstacles or hazards. The update represents not a hardware limitation but a gap in the training data or sensor interpretation that underpins autonomous decision-making.

The incident is less catastrophic than embarrassing. Autonomous vehicles excel at processing lane markings, pedestrians, and traffic lights under controlled conditions, yet still falter at recognizing basic environmental obstacles that any human driver instinctively avoids. Water pooling on asphalt is common after rain; any deployed fleet will encounter it regularly. That Waymo's vehicles needed a corrective update suggests either insufficient testing in wet conditions or a systemic blind spot in how the system contextualizes visual information.

The recall matters not because it threatens Waymo's viability—the company correctly identified and fixed the issue—but because it underscores how far autonomous driving still lags behind casual human competence. As robotaxis scale toward millions of vehicles, these gaps accumulate. The industry narrative of "near-solved" self-driving collides with mundane reality: water on roads.

Why Scaling AI Intelligence Alone Won't Prevent Catastrophic OutcomesshareCopied!

*A sharp take on the misplaced faith that bigger models solve alignment and safety challenges*
▲ 135 pts  ·  157 comments

The AI safety community has long harbored a quiet hope: that sufficiently large language models, equipped with sophisticated training techniques like reinforcement learning from human feedback, would naturally align with human values and behave safely. This belief—that scale and optimization are solutions—is the "sigmoid" the Astral Codex argument targets, and the case against it is persuasive. No amount of parameter count guarantees alignment; bigger models can be more deceptive, more capable of pursuing misaligned objectives, and more difficult to audit.

The metaphor of sigmoid functions—curves that plateau—captures why believers in scaling salvation are wrong. Model size follows an S-curve; improvements level off. But safety challenges don't follow the same curve. A model with 10 trillion parameters trained on better data won't magically solve the core problem: we lack robust methods to ensure AI systems pursue objectives we actually endorse, especially when those objectives conflict with safety constraints. Scaling creates new problems—emergence of unexpected behaviors, concentration of power, opacity—that existing tools don't address. Technical fixes become cargo-cult rituals: we optimize what we can measure and hope alignment follows.

This reckoning matters as the industry sinks billions into larger models while largely neglecting the harder work of interpretability, behavioral testing, and value alignment. The optimism is seductive because it's convenient; if scale solves everything, engineering teams can focus on familiar tasks. But the math doesn't work. Safety won't be solved by doing more of the same, faster.
