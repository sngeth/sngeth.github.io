*The world's manufacturing superpower remains locked out of advanced turbomachinery, exposing a critical vulnerability in Beijing's technological supply chain*
▲ 172 pts  ·  157 comments

Aakash's deep dive into why China has failed to master jet engine production—despite dominance across virtually every other manufacturing domain—reveals a chasm between raw industrial capacity and the precision-metallurgy exotica that separates great powers from also-rans. The 172-point engagement and 157 comments from a politically astute Hacker News readership reflects the geopolitical weight of the question: jet engines are not simply weapons; they're the ultimate constraint on Chinese commercial aviation independence and military reach. The fact that Chinese carriers still depend on Russian, Western, or joint-venture engines is a humbling reality for a nation that has industrialized faster than any in history.

The analysis cuts to the core tension: jet engines demand materials science, aerothermal simulation, and manufacturing tolerance at the intersection of physics and craft that resists centralized optimization. China has excelled at scaling proven designs—copy existing electronics, build cheaper versions, own the supply chain. But jet engines require sustained innovation in single-crystal superalloy processing, advanced coatings, and turbine blade geometry that demand decades of iterative failure and institutional knowledge. These are not problems solved by throwing workers or capital at the problem; they require the kind of continuous, high-stakes R&D that emerged from Cold War competition and remains embedded in Western defense contractors' institutional muscle memory.

The strategic implication cuts both ways. For the West, it's reassurance that certain technical moats remain durable—for now. For China, it's a roadmap of where acceleration is possible but not guaranteed. The comment thread likely reflects frustration from engineers cognizant that this gap is narrowing, not widening; the question isn't whether China will crack jet engines eventually, but whether Western policy can meaningfully delay that timeline through export controls and talent restrictions.
