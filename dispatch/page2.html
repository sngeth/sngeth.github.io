Cloudflare Blocks archive.today Over Botnet Concerns, Reigniting Questions About DNS Gatekeeping

*The flagging of the internet's most controversial archive raises thorny questions about who gets to decide what's accessible*
▲ 188 pts  ·  119 comments

**Archive.today, the scrappy web archiving service beloved by conspiracy theorists and controversial content preservationists alike, has collided with Cloudflare's threat detection.** The DNS filtering giant flagged the domain as C&C/Botnet infrastructure and delisted it from the 1.1.1.2 resolver—a move that yanks archive.today from reach for users relying on Cloudflare's privacy-focused DNS service. The 119 comments exploding across the Hacker News discussion thread reveal the ideological fissure the incident exposes: between security infrastructure providers policing traffic patterns and purists who view DNS itself as too critical to filter.

Archive.today's reputation precedes it. The service has become a destination for archiving content that mainstream platforms delete—a function that makes it simultaneously valuable to transparency advocates and useful to those spreading disproven claims. Cloudflare's classification decision, presumably algorithmic rather than editorial, sidesteps the question of intent and lands squarely on infrastructure: whether a hosting provider or DNS operator bears responsibility for what passes through its pipes. The company frames this as threat prevention; critics see it as chokepoint censorship cloaked in technical language.

The larger story is about infrastructure consolidation and the power it confers. Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1 and 1.1.1.2 DNS resolvers command hundreds of millions of queries daily. A flagging decision, automated or not, functions as a de facto blacklist that requires no transparency, no appeal process, and no judicial standard. Whether archive.today actually hosted botnet infrastructure or merely triggered pattern-matching algorithms remains opaque—and that opacity is precisely the problem.

Bayesian Statistics for the Bewildered: A Data Scientist's Field Guide to Probability's Thorniest Concept

*A new primer tackles the interpretive minefield where frequentist orthodoxy collides with prior beliefs*
▲ 126 pts  ·  32 comments

**Bayesian inference remains the statistical world's perpetual confounding problem—conceptually elegant, practically treacherous, and perpetually misunderstood by practitioners who learned frequentist methods first.** A new essay circulating among data scientists offers what amounts to a translator's guide: how to think about Bayesian approaches without fetishizing them or retreating into frequentist tradition by default. With 126 upvotes and 32 comments, the modest engagement suggests this material speaks to a specific audience—practitioners who've hit the ceiling of intuition and need scaffolding to bridge the gap.

The core difficulty hasn't changed: Bayesian statistics requires philosophical commitments that frequentist methods finesse. When you encode prior beliefs into a probability distribution, you're embedding assumptions into your mathematical apparatus. That transparency is theoretically virtuous and practically annoying—it forces you to make explicit what frequentism lets you leave vague. Most data scientists encounter Bayes through regularization, MCMC sampling, or probabilistic programming frameworks like Stan or PyMC, rarely through foundational understanding. They treat it as a toolkit rather than a worldview.

Good pedagogical writing in this domain does crucial work. It demystifies the notation, untangles conjugate priors from the actual mechanics of updating beliefs, and separates genuine insight from tribal allegiance. The essay's existence and circulation signal that demand persists for clarity in a domain where much published material assumes either complete naiveté or deep statistical sophistication—leaving the frustrated middle stranded.
