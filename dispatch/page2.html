macOS Tahoe's Menu Bar Gets a Minimalist Makeover—Here's How

*Apple's latest OS clutters your top bar; one developer found the escape hatch*
▲ 116 pts  ·  35 comments

Apple's pursuit of visual density in macOS Tahoe has left power users grimacing at an increasingly crowded menu bar—a digital strip mall of icons competing for real estate at the top of the screen. The latest iteration of the operating system loads the status area with system indicators, third-party app icons, and widgets that would make Marie Kondo weep. But a cunning workaround has emerged from the Mac enthusiast community, allowing users to surgically remove unwanted menu icons without resorting to Terminal commands or third-party utilities.

The technique, detailed on 512pixels.net, leverages macOS's built-in customization framework to hide specific icons while preserving their functionality—a distinction that matters. Rather than disabling features wholesale, users can cherry-pick which notifications and indicators deserve screen real estate. The method is straightforward enough for casual users but reveals the deeper tension between Apple's design philosophy and user autonomy: the company continues to make system-level decisions about what should be visible, forcing users into shadow customization just to reclaim their own menu bar.

With each macOS release, this cat-and-mouse game intensifies. Apple tightens the visual screws; users find new workarounds. For those already weary of the bloat, this simple trick represents a small but meaningful victory—a reminder that sometimes the most elegant solutions emerge not from corporate product teams, but from communities determined to keep their interfaces under control.

No Starch Press Returns to Spark Joy in Young Makers With Fresh Electronics Edition

*The beloved hands-on guide evolves for a new generation of tinkerers seeking tactile learning*
▲ 109 pts  ·  16 comments

No Starch Press, the publisher known for treating technical subjects with playful irreverence, has refreshed its foundational work *Electronics for Kids* with a second edition that arrives at a cultural inflection point—just as Gen Alpha's screen fatigue drives parents toward physical, buildable projects. The updated volume maintains the accessibility that made the original a gateway drug for young makers while incorporating lessons learned from a decade of evolving educational philosophy and component availability.

What distinguishes this edition is its refusal to condescend. Rather than watering down electronics fundamentals, the book deepens them, introducing concepts like circuit design and troubleshooting through experiments that reward curiosity with tangible results. Circuits light up. Motors spin. Buzzers buzz. The tactile feedback loop—cause meeting effect in real time—creates learning pathways that digital simulations simply cannot replicate. No Starch's editorial voice shines here too: humor and rigor coexist, making what could be dry technical exposition feel like an invitation into a community of makers rather than a march through a textbook.

In an era when educational publishing often chases trending buzzwords ("AI-enhanced learning," "gamified mastery"), this book's grounded approach feels almost radical. It trusts that electronics, in their fundamentals, have not changed. What has changed is the hunger among young people for things that work, that fail instructively, and that they can hold in their hands.
