Earnings Iron Condor Backtest

MSFT, META, GOOGL, AMZN — 29 earnings events over 2 years (mid-2024 to present) — 116 simulated trades — 68% real option prices from Alpaca, 32% estimated
What is this? A backtest of selling iron condors (a defined-risk options strategy) around quarterly earnings for four mega-cap tech stocks. We sell premium before earnings, hold through the announcement, and close the next morning after the volatility crush (the sharp drop in option prices once earnings uncertainty is resolved).

What does "1x expected move" mean? Before every earnings report, the options market prices in an "expected move" — how far the stock is likely to jump overnight. For example, if GOOGL is at $350 and the expected move is 5.8%, the market thinks GOOGL will land somewhere between ~$330 and ~$370 after the report. Placing short strikes at 1.0x the expected move means we sell right at that boundary — we profit if the stock moves less than the market expects. At 1.5x, we give ourselves 50% more room, which wins more often but collects less premium.

Data sources: Stock prices from Alpaca (10 years of daily bars). Earnings dates from yfinance. Option credits are currently ESTIMATED using empirical ratios — real historical option prices from Polygon.io are being fetched and will replace these estimates.

Key finding: At 1.0x the expected move, the strategy wins 98.1% of the time. But win rate alone is misleading — the losing trades can be large. How much premium you actually collect determines whether the edge is real. That's why we're fetching real historical option prices from Polygon.

How to Read These Numbers

What is delta? Delta is roughly the market's estimate of the probability that the stock will reach a given strike price. A short put delta of -0.17 means roughly a 17% chance the stock drops below that strike by expiration. Lower delta = the strike is further from the current price = the trade wins more often but collects less premium.

Why deltas differ across stocks: AMZN has the highest deltas (-0.29 / 0.32) despite having the widest strikes because the options market prices AMZN's further-out strikes relatively expensively (a "flat volatility smile"). MSFT has the lowest deltas (-0.14 / 0.18) because its further-out options are priced cheaper (a "steep smile").
What is a broken-wing condor? (META) A normal iron condor has symmetric protection — the downside and upside buffers are the same distance from the stock price. A broken-wing condor deliberately makes one side wider. For META, we pushed the downside protection further out (1.3x the expected move) while keeping the upside at 1.0x. We gave up some upside cushion to get more room on the downside — a directional tilt within an otherwise neutral trade.

Credit as % of spread width is a quick read on risk vs reward. AMZN at 52% is aggressive (generous credit, but the market thinks there's real risk). MSFT at 25% is conservative (smaller credit, higher odds of winning). Our backtest suggests the sweet spot is 30-40%, roughly where a 1.0x expected move condor lands.
