Kronos Backtest Sweep — US Equities (QQQ & SPY)

24 monthly windows per test | Finetuned vs Base model | High vs Low volatility regimes
What is this? We tested how well an AI model (Kronos) can predict QQQ (Nasdaq-100 ETF) and SPY (S&P 500 ETF) price movements by pretending we were at the end of each month over a 24-month window, asking the model "what happens next?", and then comparing its answer to what actually happened.

How it works: The model only sees past price data (open, high, low, close, volume) — no news, no earnings, no economic data. It looks at a window of recent prices and tries to predict the next set of price bars based purely on patterns it has learned.

What we're measuring:
• Direction Accuracy — Did the model correctly predict whether the price would go up or down on each bar? 50% = coin flip, higher = useful signal.
• MAE (Mean Absolute Error) — On average, how many dollars off was the predicted price from the actual price?
• MAPE (Mean Absolute % Error) — Same as MAE but as a percentage of the stock price.

Two models tested:
• Base model — The pretrained Kronos model, trained on general time-series data. No ticker-specific training.
• Finetuned model — The base model further trained specifically on ticker-specific historical prices.

Two timeframes:
• Daily — Looks at 90 days of daily prices, predicts the next 10 trading days (~2 weeks)
• 5-min — Looks at 512 five-minute bars, predicts the next 48 bars (~4 hours of trading)

Key Takeaways

Finetuning consistently hurts. The base model won every head-to-head comparison — across QQQ daily (2,587 training bars), SPY 5-min (32,487 training bars), high vol, and low vol. Even with 12x more training data, finetuning degraded performance. The model may be overfitting to training data patterns that don't generalize, or finetuning may cause the model to forget useful general knowledge from pretraining.

Direction accuracy is coin-flip territory. Across all 5 test configurations and thousands of individual predictions, direction accuracy ranged from 42% to 54%. No setup reliably exceeded 55%, which is roughly the minimum for a tradeable signal after transaction costs.

Volatility regime doesn't matter much. The model performed similarly in both calm (2017, 2021) and turbulent (2022, 2025) markets. Its best individual windows came during strong trends (sharp selloffs or sustained rallies), but on average, no regime produced reliable accuracy.

Why? QQQ and SPY are among the most liquid instruments in the world, with combined daily volume exceeding $40 billion. Thousands of professional traders and algorithms exploit any predictable price patterns until they disappear. A model that only sees past prices — no news, earnings, or macro data — is fighting against this efficiency with a significant information disadvantage.

Where might Kronos work? Less liquid instruments (small-caps, niche commodities), non-financial time series (energy demand, sensor data), ensemble signals (combining with sentiment/flow data), or regime detection (trend vs chop classification rather than price prediction).
