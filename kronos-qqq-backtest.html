QQQ Kronos Backtest Sweep

24 monthly windows (May 2024 - Apr 2026) | Finetuned vs Base model comparison
What is this? We tested how well an AI model (Kronos) can predict QQQ (Nasdaq-100 ETF) price movements by pretending we were at the end of each month over the past 2 years, asking the model "what happens next?", and then comparing its answer to what actually happened.

How it works: The model only sees past price data (open, high, low, close, volume) — no news, no earnings, no economic data. It looks at a window of recent prices and tries to predict the next set of price bars based purely on patterns it has learned.

What we're measuring:
• Direction Accuracy — Did the model correctly predict whether the price would go up or down on each bar? 50% = coin flip, higher = useful signal.
• MAE (Mean Absolute Error) — On average, how many dollars off was the predicted price from the actual price?
• MAPE (Mean Absolute % Error) — Same as MAE but as a percentage of the stock price.

Two models tested:
• Base model — The pretrained Kronos model, trained on general time-series data. No QQQ-specific training.
• Finetuned model — The base model further trained specifically on QQQ historical prices to specialize it.

Two timeframes:
• Daily — Looks at 90 days of daily prices, predicts the next 10 trading days (~2 weeks)
• 5-min — Looks at 512 five-minute bars, predicts the next 48 bars (~4 hours of trading)

Key Takeaways

Daily Model (predicting ~2 weeks ahead): The finetuned model got direction right 43% of the time, and the base model 48%. Both are worse than a coin flip (50%). Teaching the model QQQ-specific patterns didn't help — it actually made things slightly worse. This is likely because 2,587 daily bars isn't enough data for the model to learn meaningful patterns beyond what it already knows.

5-Min Model (predicting ~4 hours ahead): The base model got direction right 51.2% of the time across 1,081 individual up/down predictions. The price error is small ($2.80 on average) because 5-minute moves are tiny, but the model is essentially guessing on direction.

What does this mean? If you flipped a coin to decide "will QQQ go up or down?", you'd be right about as often as this model. A useful trading signal would need to be consistently above 55-60% to overcome transaction costs and be worth acting on.

Why? QQQ is one of the most liquid, heavily-traded instruments in the world. Thousands of professional traders and algorithms are already exploiting any predictable price patterns, which means those patterns get arbitraged away almost immediately. A model that only looks at past prices is fighting against this efficiency. The model may perform better on less liquid, less-followed instruments where patterns persist longer.
